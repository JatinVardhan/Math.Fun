Basic Arithmetic Operations

Basic arithmetic operations include addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division. These operations are fundamental in mathematics and form the basis for more complex mathematical concepts.


Example:

For example, the sum of 5 and 3 is 8, the difference between 7 and 4 is 3, the product of 2 and 6 is 12, and the quotient of 9 divided by 3 is 3.


Interesting Facts:

1. The basic operations are the foundation for solving mathematical problems and real-world applications.
2. The order of operations, often remembered by the acronym PEMDAS, dictates the sequence in which operations should be performed.
3. Arithmetic operations are used extensively in computer programming and scientific calculations.

BODMAS

BODMAS is an acronym that represents the order of operations in mathematics: Brackets, Orders (i.e., powers and square roots, etc.), Division and Multiplication (from left to right), and Addition and Subtraction (from left to right).


Example:

To evaluate an expression like 3 + 5 × 2, BODMAS guides us to perform the multiplication first and then the addition, resulting in 13.


Interesting Facts:

1. BODMAS ensures a standardized way of solving mathematical expressions to avoid ambiguity.
2. Following BODMAS is crucial to obtaining accurate and consistent results in calculations.
3. BODMAS is a fundamental concept taught in mathematics education worldwide.

Square & Square Root

The square of a number is the result of multiplying the number with itself.

Square root of a number is a value, which on multiplication by itself, gives the original number. The square root is an inverse method of squaring a number. Hence, squares and square roots are related concepts.


Example:

The square of 4 is 4 × 4 = 16, and the square root of 16 is 4.


Interesting Facts:

1. Perfect squares are numbers that have integer square roots.
2. The square root of 2 is an irrational number, meaning it cannot be expressed as a fraction.
3. Squaring and square roots are fundamental in geometry, physics, and engineering.

Exponent & Modulo

In mathematics, power defines a base number raised to the exponent, where base number is the factor which is multiplied by itself and exponent denotes the number of times the same base number is multiplied.

Modulo is the remainder when one number is divided by another.


Example:

In the expression 2^3, 2 is raised to the power of 3, resulting in 2 × 2 × 2 = 8. The modulo of 7 divided by 3 is 1, as 7 = 3 × 2 + 1.


Interesting Facts:

1. Exponents are used in scientific notation, where numbers are expressed as a product of a coefficient and a power of 10.
2. Modulo has applications in computer science, cryptography, and number theory.
3. Exponents and modulo are essential concepts in algebra and discrete mathematics.

Logarithm & Factorial

Logarithms measure the exponent to which a base must be raised to obtain a certain number. Factorial is the product of all positive integers up to a given number.


Example:

The logarithm of 100 to the base 10 is 2, as 10^2 = 100. The factorial of 5 is 5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1 = 120.


Interesting Facts:

1. Logarithms are useful for solving exponential equations and are common in fields like physics and engineering.
2. Factorials are used in combinatorics and probability theory.
3. Both logarithms and factorials have applications in algorithms and computer science.

Trigonometry Functions

Trigonometry is the branch of mathematics that studies the relationships between the angles and sides of triangles. Common trigonometric functions include sine, cosine, tangent, cosecant, secant, and cotangent.


Example:

In a right-angled triangle, the sine of an angle is the ratio of the length of the side opposite the angle to the hypotenuse.


Interesting Facts:

1. Trigonometry is widely used in physics, engineering, and computer graphics.
2. The unit circle is a fundamental concept in trigonometry.
3. Trigonometric functions have applications in modeling periodic phenomena.
