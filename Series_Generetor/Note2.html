Factors

Factors are the building blocks of any number. Every positive integer can be expressed as a product of prime numbers, and these prime numbers are its prime factors.


Example:

The prime factorization of 24 is 2 x 2 x 2 x 3 or 2^3 x 3, where 2 and 3 are prime numbers.


Interesting Facts:

1. The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic states that every positive integer has a unique prime factorization.
2. Prime factorization is crucial in number theory and cryptography.
3. The Sieve of Eratosthenes is an ancient algorithm for finding all prime numbers up to any given limit.

Fibonacci Series

The Fibonacci series is a sequence of numbers in which each number is the sum of the two preceding ones, usually starting with 0 and 1. It has widespread applications in mathematics and nature.


Example:

The Fibonacci series starts: 0, 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21, and so on. Each number is the sum of the two preceding numbers.


Interesting Facts:

1. Fibonacci numbers appear in various natural phenomena, such as the arrangement of leaves on a stem or the spiral of a nautilus shell.
2. The ratio of consecutive Fibonacci numbers converges to the golden ratio, approximately 1.618.
3. Fibonacci numbers are used in algorithms for searching and sorting.

Arithmetic Progression (AP) Series

Arithmetic Progression (AP) is a sequence of numbers in which the difference of any two successive members is a constant. This constant difference is known as the "common difference."


Example:

In the AP series 2, 5, 8, 11, 14, the common difference is 3. Each term is obtained by adding 3 to the previous term.


Interesting Facts:

1. The general form of an AP series is given by a, a+d, a+2d, a+3d, and so on.
2. AP series is widely used in mathematics, physics, and finance.
3. The sum of the first n terms of an AP series can be calculated using specific formulas. 

            
                 i.e.  Sn = n/2 [2a + (n-1)d]

            n represents the number of terms of AP
            a is the first term of the AP
            d is the common difference of AP

GP Series (Geometric Progression)

Geometric Progression (GP) is a sequence of numbers in which the ratio of any two successive members is a constant. This constant ratio is known as the "common ratio."


Example:

In the GP series 2, 6, 18, 54, 162, the common ratio is 3. Each term is obtained by multiplying the previous term by 3.


Interesting Facts:

1. The general form of a GP series is given by a, ar, ar^2, ar^3, and so on.
2. GP series is used in various fields, including physics, biology, and finance.
3. The sum of the first n terms of a GP series can be calculated using specific formulas.
